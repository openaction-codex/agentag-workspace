# Research method

Use this as a routing checklist, not a requirement to fill every category.

## Source hierarchy

Prefer sources in this order:

1. Official organization websites, reports, accounts, policies, procurement
   documents, press releases, job postings, and public statements.
2. Government, court, company, charity, association, grant, lobbying,
   transparency, and public-procurement registries.
3. Official partner, funder, network, conference, and supplier disclosures.
4. Reputable news and specialist publications with named reporting.
5. Technology-detection services, professional profiles, directories, and
   aggregators as discovery leads that require corroboration.

Use search snippets only to locate a source. Do not cite a snippet when the
underlying page is available. Prefer sources from the last 24 months for current
strategy and technology, while using older primary sources for history.

## Entity and organization

Establish:

- official and trading names, country, legal form, registration identifiers;
- official domains and relevant subdomains;
- parent, subsidiary, foundation, chapter, campaign, caucus, or federation
  relationships;
- mission, constituencies, programs, geography, and languages;
- governance and operating structure relevant to purchasing or deployment.

Watch for separate entities sharing a brand or separate brands sharing an
entity. Record contradictions rather than choosing the most convenient answer.

## Scale and operating model

Look for supported indicators such as:

- members, donors, supporters, subscribers, volunteers, staff, offices, local
  chapters, affiliates, territories, websites, campaigns, and events;
- centralized versus federated governance;
- staff or volunteer dependence;
- recurring campaigns, elections, congresses, fundraising drives, petitions,
  consultations, or membership renewals;
- multilingual and multi-country operations.

Label self-reported numbers as such and include their reporting period.

## Strategy and buying signals

Prioritize observable changes that can create a timely need:

- new leadership or a reorganization;
- a new campaign, program, geography, chapter network, or membership drive;
- website redesign, migration, rebrand, accessibility effort, or domain change;
- hiring related to CRM, data, digital, fundraising, communications, security,
  membership, organizing, or IT;
- public procurement, grant award, budget cycle, election, congress, launch, or
  contract renewal;
- data incident, deliverability problem, platform outage, compliance concern,
  or explicit tool dissatisfaction;
- new integration, supplier, or partnership.

Do not manufacture urgency from ordinary activity. Include the signal date and
explain why it could matter to OpenAction.

## Finances and purchasing capacity

Search applicable official registries and the organization's own filings for:

- annual revenue/income and expenditure;
- assets, reserves, debt, profit/loss, or cash when relevant;
- grants, public subsidies, donations, membership dues, campaign funding, and
  major contracts;
- procurement rules, active tenders, framework agreements, and purchasing
  thresholds;
- fiscal year and reporting period.

Always state currency, period, consolidation scope, and whether a figure is
filed, self-reported, press-reported, estimated, or a proxy. Never convert a
grant, total campaign spend, assets, group revenue, or fundraising target into
an assumed software budget. If accounts are unavailable, describe only concrete
capacity proxies and mark capacity `Unknown`.

## Digital estate and tools

Inspect public pages and policies for evidence of:

- CMS and website hosting;
- CRM, membership, fundraising, donation, petition, event, volunteer, survey,
  and form systems;
- email, SMS, marketing automation, and transactional messaging;
- payments and donation processors;
- analytics, consent management, tag management, and advertising tools;
- authentication, member areas, data portals, APIs, and integrations;
- separate regional or campaign websites and inconsistent tooling.

Evidence strength:

- **High:** vendor named by the organization; supplier contract; public
  configuration; product-specific hosted domain; authoritative case study.
- **Medium:** multiple consistent technical artifacts or current integration
  evidence.
- **Low:** a single detector, script, cookie name, DNS hint, old job posting, or
  undated directory entry.

Client-visible detection misses server-side systems and may retain traces of
unused tools. Report `Observed`, `Likely`, or `Possible`; never present a
detector's guess as confirmed deployment.

## Stakeholders

Find and rank up to three publicly stated professional contacts relevant to the
possible purchase. Search official team and governance pages, recent reports,
press releases, job announcements, event speaker pages, professional profiles,
partner/network pages, and public procurement documents.

Look for complementary buying roles:

- executive or elected sponsor;
- communications, digital, organizing, membership, fundraising, data, IT,
  security, finance, procurement, or operations owner;
- likely users, technical evaluators, budget owners, and external advisers;
- partners who may offer a credible warm route.

Rank candidates using account-specific evidence:

1. **Problem relevance:** Does the role own the likely pain or initiative?
2. **Buying influence:** Can the person champion, evaluate, fund, or approve a
   platform decision?
3. **Signal relevance:** Is the person connected to the observed reason to act?
4. **Currentness:** Is there recent evidence that they still hold the role?
5. **Reachability:** Is there an official professional route or warm path?

Do not default to the chief executive when a digital, communications,
membership, organizing, fundraising, or operations leader is the more credible
champion. Do not guess authority from title alone. Explain the likely buying
role as a supported inference and what must be validated.

Acceptable routes include an official work email published by the organization,
an official contact or team page, a current professional profile, or a specific
public partner/network connection. Never infer email patterns, report personal
contact details, or rely solely on a people-search/enrichment database.

When no named person is reliably identifiable, return the exact role/team to
target and the organization's official route. Do not lower the evidence bar to
force three names.

## Competitive and alternative context

Identify confirmed incumbent suppliers and plausible alternatives, including:

- separate point tools and manual processes;
- internal development or an agency-managed stack;
- incumbent suites or sector-specific platforms;
- doing nothing or postponing consolidation.

Avoid unsupported competitor criticism. Explain the trade-off relevant to the
account: consolidation, autonomy, implementation, integration, sovereignty,
cost, migration risk, or organizational change.

## Evidence ledger

For research notes, track:

| Claim | Status | Confidence | Source | Published/updated | Accessed |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Concise claim | Confirmed fact / Supported inference / Unknown | High / Medium / Low | Direct URL | Date or unknown | Current date |

Consequential claims include identity, scale, finances, current tools, recent
signals, stakeholder roles, and anything used to justify fit or outreach.
